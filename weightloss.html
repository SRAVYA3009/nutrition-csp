Taking Meals on Time

  • A balanced and nutritious diet in combination of exercise or light physical activity is surely the key to healthy life.
  • Many of these daily fluctuations are related to circadian rhythm, the cycle that moderates our sleep-wake patterns over 24 hours. In other words, it’s the body’s internal clock, and it reacts primarily to changes in light
  • We usually think of circadian rhythms as affecting how tired or awake we feel, but they also influence other physical, mental, and behavioral processes in the body, including eating and digestion
  • Conversely, mealtimes influence circadian rhythm. Thus, our eating habits and circadian rhythms constantly interact, though some scientists remain unsure as to exactly how much
  • Still, researchers have found associations between circadian rhythm, mealtimes, weight status, and even insulin resistance, a hallmark of metabolic conditions like obesity and diabetes

Foods that Help Heal Your Digestive System

  • Fennel

    • The seeds of this Mediterranean herb have been used as traditional digestive medicine for centuries. Ground-up fennel seeds are prescribed for many digestive ailments, and the essential oil of fennel is often used to help with bloating.

  • Ginger

    • Ginger has a long history of medicinal use in cultures around the world. Its power to help with nausea and vomiting has long been known. Research backs the value of ginger as a promoter of digestive health, as well.

  • Onion

    • Onions are a great way to help create a healthy environment for good gut bacteria. The vegetable contains inulin, a substance that even helps prevent colon cancer. Joining a fitness class or just doing workouts together can be great fun and a great way to spend time together.



  • Papaya

    • Papaya is valuable as a promoter of digestive health because it is rich in an enzyme called papain. The enzyme is able to help with bloating, constipation, heartburn and indigestion. It can even help as a digestive after a heavy, protein-rich meal.

  • Pineapple

    • Papaya isn't the only fruit that contains digestive enzymes. Pineapple is a rich in bromelain, another enzyme that helps in the digestion of protein. The bromelain found in pineapple is even helpful in the treatment of indigestion, and in fighting the effects of harmful bacteria such as E. coli.

  • Salmon

    • Salmon is great for your digestion because it is rich in omega-3 fatty acids, substances that help with inflammation. If you suffer from inflammatory bowel disease, or any other digestive disorder that inflames your gut, the omega-3 fatty acids in salmon can help.

  • Sauerkraut

    • This traditional German recipe for fermented cabbage can quickly improve digestive health, and help you fight flatulence. In some cases, it may even help you with the symptoms of irritable bowel syndrome.

  • Yogurt

    • Fermented foods, in general, tend to help with digestive health. Yogurt, which is fermented milk, is rich in friendly bacteria known as probiotics and can help with everything from constipation to diarrhea and bloating.

  • Coconut water

    • This refreshing drink is not only consumed for its health-boosting nutrients, but is also purported to have some medicinal uses. As multifunctional as it is, its also no surprise that coconut water has become the rehydration choice for many athletes around the world.

  • According to a review article by Alexia Prades and co-workers, published in Fruits in 2012, coconut water was traditionally used in Indian Ayurvedic to treat ailments such as hair loss, burns and heart problems. Other traditional uses included: Treating burning pain during urination, Dysuria, Gastritis, Burning pain of the eyes, Indigestion, Hiccups.
